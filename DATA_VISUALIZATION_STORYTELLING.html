📊 Data Visualization & Storytelling

How to Turn Numbers into Meaningful Stories

A Comprehensive Lesson Plan for 8th Grade & Beyond

🎯 What's This All About?

Every day, companies, scientists, and news organizations deal with millions of data points. But raw numbers don't mean much to most people. What matters is the story behind those numbers.

Think about it:

  • Netflix doesn't just store numbers about what you watch—they use it to tell you what you might like
  • A doctor doesn't just look at your temperature—the trend tells them if you're getting better or worse
  • A sports commentator doesn't just say "Player A has 28 points"—they explain what that means for the game

Data visualization is the art and science of turning raw data into pictures and charts that tell a story. In this lesson, you'll learn:

  • The math behind different chart types
  • How to choose the right visualization for your data
  • How to tell a compelling story with data
  • How to write Python code that creates visualizations
  • How to spot misleading visualizations (it happens more than you think!)

📖 Part 1: Understanding Data Stories

What Makes a Good Data Story?

A good data story has three parts:

Part What It Does Example
Setup Establishes the context—why should anyone care? "Did you know most teenagers sleep less than 8 hours?"
Conflict Reveals the problem or interesting pattern "Students who sleep less get worse grades"
Resolution Suggests action or deeper understanding "Schools should start later to help students sleep more"

Real Example: The Smartphone Story

Setup: "Teenagers use their phones a lot. But how much?"

Visualization: A line chart showing average daily screen time increasing from 2010 to 2024

Conflict: "While screen time increased, average grades decreased and anxiety increased"

Visualization: A scatter plot showing the correlation

Resolution: "This doesn't prove phones cause worse grades, but it's worth asking: How does technology affect our lives?"

The Four Types of Data Stories

  1. Change Over Time - "Something is getting better or worse"

    Examples: Temperature rising, social media followers growing, battery draining

  2. Comparison - "This is bigger/smaller/better/worse than that"

    Examples: Which sport is most popular, which country has the most population, which pizza topping wins

  3. Composition - "Here's what makes up the whole"

    Examples: How your day breaks down (school, sleep, homework, fun), what's in your budget, types of music you listen to

  4. Relationship - "These two things are connected"

    Examples: Study time vs. grades, exercise vs. energy, sugar intake vs. weight

💡 Key Insight: Before you create ANY visualization, ask yourself: "What story am I trying to tell?" Everything else follows from that.

📊 Part 2: The Math Behind Charts

Understanding Axes and Coordinates

Most charts use two axes: horizontal (X) and vertical (Y). This is called a coordinate system.

The Basics

  • X-axis (horizontal): Usually your independent variable (time, categories, or what you're measuring)
  • Y-axis (vertical): Usually your dependent variable (the thing you're tracking)
  • Point: A specific location on the graph given by coordinates (X, Y)

Example: If you plot "Days" on X and "Messages Sent" on Y:

  • Day 1, 15 messages = point at (1, 15)
  • Day 2, 22 messages = point at (2, 22)
  • Day 3, 18 messages = point at (3, 18)

Four Essential Math Concepts

1. Mean (Average)

What it is: The typical value. Add all numbers and divide by how many there are.

Formula: Mean = (Sum of all values) ÷ (Number of values)

# In Python: data = [5, 10, 15, 20, 30] mean = sum(data) / len(data) print(f"Mean: {mean}") # Output: 16.0

Why it matters: Gives you a quick sense of "typical." If average grade is 85%, that's a typical performance.

2. Median (Middle Value)

What it is: The middle value when you line up all numbers from smallest to largest.

Example: In the list [3, 5, 7, 9, 11], the median is 7 (right in the middle)

# In Python: import statistics data = [3, 5, 7, 9, 11] median = statistics.median(data) print(f"Median: {median}") # Output: 7

Why it matters: Better than mean when you have extreme values. If salaries are [$30k, $35k, $40k, $200k], median ($37.5k) is more realistic than mean ($76.25k)

3. Percentage

What it is: A part of a whole expressed out of 100.

Formula: (Part ÷ Whole) × 100 = Percentage

# In Python: total_students = 200 students_passing = 150 percentage = (students_passing / total_students) * 100 print(f"Pass rate: {percentage}%") # Output: 75.0%

Why it matters: Makes it easy to compare. "150 out of 200 passed" sounds good, but "75% passed" is clearer.

4. Correlation

What it is: A measure of how closely two variables move together. Ranges from -1 to +1.

  • +1 or close to it: Perfect positive correlation (both increase together)
  • 0: No correlation (they're independent)
  • -1 or close to it: Perfect negative correlation (one increases, other decreases)
# In Python: import numpy as np study_hours = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5] test_scores = [60, 70, 75, 85, 90] correlation = np.corrcoef(study_hours, test_scores)[0, 1] print(f"Correlation: {correlation:.2f}") # Output: 0.99

Why it matters: Tells you if a pattern is real or just coincidence. Correlation ≠ Causation (more sleep correlates with better grades, but doesn't necessarily CAUSE better grades)

📈 Part 3: Five Major Visualization Types

When to Use Each Type

1. Line Charts - Showing Trends Over Time

Best for: Tracking how something changes (stock prices, temperature, population growth)

The story: "Is this going up, down, or staying the same?"

# Python: Basic line chart import matplotlib.pyplot as plt months = ['Jan', 'Feb', 'Mar', 'Apr', 'May', 'Jun'] revenue = [1000, 1500, 1200, 2000, 2200, 2500] plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.plot(months, revenue, marker='o', linewidth=2, color='#3498db') plt.title('Monthly Revenue Trend', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') plt.xlabel('Month') plt.ylabel('Revenue ($)') plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3) plt.show()
Revenue $2500 | ● | / \ | / ● | / ● |/ / $1000 | ● |_________________ Jan Feb Mar Apr May

The story: "Revenue took a dip in March, but recovered and shows solid growth overall"

2. Bar Charts - Making Comparisons

Best for: Comparing categories (which movie made the most money, which city has the largest population)

The story: "Which one is the biggest?"

# Python: Bar chart comparison import matplotlib.pyplot as plt categories = ['Red Team', 'Blue Team', 'Green Team', 'Yellow Team'] scores = [85, 92, 78, 88] colors = ['red', 'blue', 'green', 'gold'] plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.bar(categories, scores, color=colors, alpha=0.7, edgecolor='black') plt.title('Team Scores Comparison', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') plt.ylabel('Score') plt.ylim(0, 100) for i, v in enumerate(scores): plt.text(i, v + 1, str(v), ha='center', fontweight='bold') plt.show()
Score 100 | 92 | █ 88 | █ █ 85 | █ █ █ █ |_█_____█____█___█_ Red Blue Green Yel

The story: "Blue Team dominates, scoring 92. Red Team is a close second. Green Team needs improvement."

3. Pie Charts - Showing Parts of a Whole

Best for: Showing percentages or portions (budget breakdown, market share, energy sources)

The story: "What makes up the whole?"

# Python: Pie chart import matplotlib.pyplot as plt activities = ['Sleep', 'School', 'Homework', 'Friends', 'Gaming'] hours = [8, 7, 2, 3, 4] colors = ['#3498db', '#e74c3c', '#f39c12', '#2ecc71', '#9b59b6'] plt.figure(figsize=(10, 8)) plt.pie(hours, labels=activities, autopct='%1.1f%%', colors=colors, startangle=90) plt.title('How I Spend My Day', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') plt.show()

The story: "My day is fairly balanced. Sleep takes up 33%, school takes 29%, and the rest is split between homework, friends, and gaming. That's healthy!"

4. Scatter Plots - Showing Relationships

Best for: Revealing correlations between two variables

The story: "Are these two things connected?"

# Python: Scatter plot with trend import matplotlib.pyplot as plt import numpy as np study_hours = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7] test_scores = [60, 65, 70, 78, 85, 88, 92] plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.scatter(study_hours, test_scores, s=100, alpha=0.6, color='#16a085') # Add a trend line z = np.polyfit(study_hours, test_scores, 1) p = np.poly1d(z) plt.plot(study_hours, p(study_hours), "r--", linewidth=2, label='Trend') plt.title('Study Hours vs. Test Scores', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') plt.xlabel('Hours Studied') plt.ylabel('Test Score (%)') plt.legend() plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3) plt.show()
Score 92 | ● 85 | ● 78 | ● 70 | ● 65 | ● 60 | ● |_●_____________ 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 Study Hours

The story: "There's a clear relationship: more study time = better scores. The data shows a strong positive correlation."

5. Histograms - Showing Distribution

Best for: Showing how data is distributed across ranges (heights of students, test score distribution)

The story: "How is this spread out?"

# Python: Histogram import matplotlib.pyplot as plt test_scores = [55, 60, 65, 70, 72, 75, 78, 80, 82, 85, 88, 90, 92, 95] plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6)) plt.hist(test_scores, bins=7, color='#9b59b6', alpha=0.7, edgecolor='black') plt.title('Distribution of Test Scores', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold') plt.xlabel('Score Range') plt.ylabel('Number of Students') plt.show()

The story: "Most students scored in the 70-95 range. Only a few scored below 70. The class is generally doing well!"

🎬 Part 4: Telling Stories with Data - The Advanced Technique

The Five-Step Story Framework

Step 1: Ask a Question

Start with something you want to know. This becomes your story's core question.

Examples:

  • "Does practicing make us better at video games?"
  • "Are students who participate in sports happier?"
  • "Which streaming service is most popular in our school?"

Step 2: Gather Data

Collect the numbers that will answer your question.

Methods: Surveys, observations, existing datasets, experiments

Example: Survey 50 students about streaming services, hours of exercise, and happiness levels

Step 3: Analyze the Data

Calculate means, medians, correlations, and trends to understand what the numbers reveal.

What to look for: Patterns, outliers, surprises, connections

Step 4: Choose the Right Visualization

Pick a chart type that best shows what you discovered.

Question: "What's the best way to show this so others understand?"

Step 5: Tell the Story

Add context, labels, and commentary to turn the visualization into a narrative.

Structure: Why should we care? What does the data show? What does it mean?

Real Example: The "Social Media and Sleep" Story

🎯 The Question

"Does social media use affect how much sleep students get?"

📊 The Data

Student Daily Social Media (hours) Sleep (hours)
Alex 1 9
Jordan 3 7
Casey 5 5
Sam 6 4
Riley 2 8

🔍 The Analysis

  • Average social media use: 3.4 hours
  • Average sleep: 6.6 hours (less than the recommended 8-10)
  • Correlation: -0.95 (very strong negative correlation)
  • Insight: Students using more social media sleep less

📈 The Visualization

A scatter plot showing the relationship, with the trend line clearly showing the negative correlation.

💭 The Story

"Here's what we found: Our survey of 700 students shows a powerful pattern. Students who spend 1-2 hours on social media average 8-9 hours of sleep—healthy! We're not saying social media CAUSES less sleep—there could be other factors. But if you're struggling to get enough sleep, this data suggests limiting screen time might help."

Story Principles That Work

✅ DO These Things

  • Start with the big idea, then show the data
  • Use honest, accurate numbers
  • Acknowledge limitations ("only surveyed 50 students")
  • Use colors strategically (highlight important data)
  • Include units (dollars, hours, percentages)
  • Let the data speak for itself first

❌ DON'T Do These Things

  • Manipulate axes to exaggerate small changes
  • Cherry-pick data that supports your opinion
  • Claim causation when you only have correlation
  • Use too many colors (confuses viewers)
  • Forget to label your axes and title
  • Show data without context

Complete Data Story Example in Python

Project: School Lunch Preferences

The story: "What are students' favorite lunch options? And does grade level affect preferences?"

# Complete Python data visualization example import matplotlib.pyplot as plt import numpy as np # Step 1: Define our data foods = ['Pizza', 'Tacos', 'Salad', 'Burger', 'Sushi'] 6th_grade = [45, 38, 12, 42, 8] 7th_grade = [42, 35, 18, 40, 15] 8th_grade = [38, 40, 25, 38, 20] # Step 2: Create figure with multiple subplots fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(15, 6)) # First chart: Overall preferences (pie chart) total_votes = [sum(x) for x in zip(6th_grade, 7th_grade, 8th_grade)] colors = ['#e74c3c', '#f39c12', '#2ecc71', '#3498db', '#9b59b6'] axes[0].pie(total_votes, labels=foods, autopct='%1.1f%%', colors=colors, startangle=90) axes[0].set_title('Overall Lunch Preferences\n(All Grades Combined)', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold') # Second chart: By grade level (bar chart) x = np.arange(len(foods)) width = 0.25 axes[1].bar(x - width, 6th_grade, width, label='6th Grade', alpha=0.8) axes[1].bar(x, 7th_grade, width, label='7th Grade', alpha=0.8) axes[1].bar(x + width, 8th_grade, width, label='8th Grade', alpha=0.8) axes[1].set_xlabel('Food Type') axes[1].set_ylabel('Number of Votes') axes[1].set_title('Lunch Preferences by Grade Level', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold') axes[1].set_xticks(x) axes[1].set_xticklabels(foods) axes[1].legend() axes[1].grid(True, alpha=0.3, axis='y') plt.tight_layout() plt.show() # Step 3: Analyze the story print("📊 LUNCH PREFERENCE ANALYSIS") print("=" * 40) for i, food in enumerate(foods): total = total_votes[i] percentage = (total / sum(total_votes)) * 100 print(f"{food}: {total} votes ({percentage:.1f}%)") print("\n💡 THE STORY:") print("Pizza is the clear winner with 36% of votes.") print("Interesting: 8th graders prefer Sushi (20% vs 8% for 6th graders).") print("Salad is least popular overall, but gaining among older students.")

⚠️ Part 6: Spotting Bad Data Visualizations

Not all charts tell the truth. Sometimes it's intentional, sometimes it's accidental. Here's how to spot when a visualization is misleading:

❌ Problem 1: Manipulated Axis

Bad: A chart showing sales increase from $1,000,000 to $1,000,100 looks like a huge spike because the Y-axis doesn't start at 0.

Good: Always start your Y-axis at 0 (or clearly state if you didn't).

❌ Problem 2: Correlation ≠ Causation

Bad: "Ice cream sales correlate with drowning deaths, so ice cream causes drowning!"

Reality: Both happen more in summer. Correlation doesn't mean one causes the other.

❌ Problem 3: Cherry-Picked Data

Bad: "This social media platform is growing!" (Shows only months where it grew, ignores months where it shrank)

Good: Show all relevant time periods for context.

❌ Problem 4: Comparing Incomparable Things

Bad: "Country A has 100 million people, Country B has 10 million. A is 10x bigger!" (But A might be 100x wealthier for a different reason)

Good: Only compare things that are actually comparable, or explain the context.

❌ Problem 5: Missing Information

Bad: A bar chart with no title, no labels, just numbers

Good: Always include title, axis labels, units (dollars, hours, etc.), and data source

🕵️ When you see a data visualization, ask:
  • Is the source reliable?
  • Are all relevant time periods shown?
  • Are the axes manipulated?
  • Is someone confusing correlation with causation?
  • Am I comparing apples to apples?
