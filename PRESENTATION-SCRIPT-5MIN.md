## FLOW: 5 slides, 1 minute each, Setup-Conflict-Resolution

### SLIDE 1 — THE HOOK (Ismael, 60 seconds)
**[Show: StorageScout hero stats bar]**

> "What if I told you that one technology could cut blackouts, slash energy bills, and create thousands of jobs — and most states are barely using it?

> We're talking about battery storage. The ability to store energy from solar and wind so you can use it when the sun goes down or the wind stops.

> Here's where we are right now: The US has 26 gigawatts of battery storage. Sounds like a lot — but it's only 2.3% of our total power capacity. We need 400 gigawatts by 2050 to hit clean energy goals. We're at 6% of where we need to be.

> Battery costs have dropped 97% since 1991 — from $7,500 per kilowatt-hour down to $139. The technology is ready. The question is: which states are actually building it?"

### SLIDE 2 — THE DATA: WHO'S WINNING (Kevin, 60 seconds)
**[Show: StorageScout "Top States by Storage Capacity" chart]**

> "We pulled data from the EIA — the US Energy Information Administration — and ranked every state by battery storage capacity. Here's what stands out.

> California leads with 10.9 gigawatts. That's more than the next 5 states combined. Texas is second with 5.5 gigawatts. Then you've got Arizona, Nevada, Florida — all sun-heavy states making the investment.

> But here's the real story: look at New York. 1.8 gigawatts. 4th largest economy in the country, 19 million people, and they're 6th in storage. Illinois — 0.5 gigawatts. They have nuclear plants that could charge batteries all night, and they're barely in the game.

> The top 5 states hold 75% of all US battery storage. The other 45 states are splitting the remaining 25%."

**[Click to next slide]**

---

### SLIDE 3 — THE CONFLICT: WHY IT MATTERS (Juan, 60 seconds)
**[Show: StorageScout "The Gap: Storage vs Emissions" chart]**

> "So why should anyone care which states have batteries?

> Because without storage, renewable energy has a fatal flaw — it only works when the sun shines or the wind blows. At 7 PM when everyone gets home, turns on the AC, starts cooking — that's peak demand. And that's exactly when solar drops to zero.

> What fills that gap right now? Natural gas. Coal. The dirtiest, most expensive power on the grid.

> This chart shows it: states with more storage have lower emissions per capita. California's carbon intensity dropped 40% in 10 years — and battery storage is a huge reason why. Meanwhile, states with almost no storage — West Virginia, Wyoming, North Dakota — are still burning coal because they have no way to store clean energy for peak hours.

> The gap between states that invest in storage and states that don't is turning into an economic gap too."

**[Click to next slide]**

---

### SLIDE 4 — THE RESOLUTION: WHAT CHANGES (Ismael, 60 seconds)
**[Show: StorageScout "What-If Simulator" — slide the slider to 100GW]**

> "So what happens if we actually build the storage we need?

> This simulator lets you see it. Right now we're at 26 gigawatts. Watch what happens when I slide to 100 gigawatts — that's a realistic 2035 target.

> Emissions drop by 180 million metric tons — that's like taking 39 million cars off the road. Every American car in California, Texas, AND New York — gone.

> 420,000 new jobs. Not Silicon Valley jobs — installation, manufacturing, electrical work. Jobs you can get with a certification, not a 4-year degree.

> $45 billion in energy savings. Stored solar at $34 per megawatt-hour is cheaper than ANY fossil fuel plant running today.

> This isn't future technology. California already proved it works. The question is whether the other 49 states catch up."

**[Click to next slide]**

### SLIDE 5 — THE CLOSE (Kevin, 60 seconds)
**[Show: StorageScout "Why This Is Happening: The Cost Collapse" chart]**

> "Let's end with the economics — because this is what makes it inevitable.

> In 1991, a lithium-ion battery cost $7,500 per kilowatt-hour. Today: $139. That's a 97% cost drop. Solar panels followed the same curve — down 99% since 1976.

> We're past the tipping point. New solar plus battery storage is already cheaper than running an existing coal plant. Not building a new one — running one that's already built and paid for.

> The two fastest-growing jobs in America according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics? Wind turbine technician — 60% growth. Solar installer — 48% growth. Both in energy. Both accessible without a 4-year degree.

> The state that stores the most energy wins. Lower bills, cleaner air, more jobs. The data is clear. The only question left is: which states are going to act on it?

> Thank you. Our live dashboard is at storagescout on GitHub Pages if you want to explore the data yourself."

## IF THEY ASK QUESTIONS
- **"Where's the data from?"** → EIA (Energy Information Administration), IRENA, BLS (Bureau of Labor Statistics)
- **"What about nuclear?"** → Nuclear is great baseload but can't ramp up/down fast. Storage complements it — charge batteries at night from nuclear, discharge at peak.
- **"Isn't this expensive?"** → New storage is cheaper than running existing coal. $139/kWh vs $68/MWh coal. Economics already won.
- **"What about other countries?"** → China has 70% of global battery manufacturing. US is catching up with IRA funding. This is also a competitiveness issue.
- **"Why not just use more solar?"** → Solar only works 6-8 hours/day. Storage is what makes it a 24/7 power source. Without storage, solar is a daytime-only solution.
