💻 Learn to Show Your Data!

A Data Visualization Lesson Plan for Beginners

🎯 What Is This Class About?

Have you ever wondered how to show information in a cool, visual way? Like how Netflix shows your watch history with a line going up and down, or how a pie chart shows what kinds of pizza people like?

In this class, you'll learn to create 5 different types of visual charts. Each person in the group will become an expert in ONE type of chart. By the end, you'll combine everything into a mini dashboard (a page with all your charts together).

📚 New Word: A visualization is a picture or chart that shows information instead of just using numbers and words.

👥 Meet Your 5 Chart Experts

PERSON 1

📈 Trends Over Time

Your superpower: Show how something changes day by day, week by week, or month by month.

What you'll create: A LINE CHART (like a video game health bar that goes up and down)

Real-life examples you know:

  • Your grade getting better as the semester goes on
  • Temperature going from cold to hot during the day
  • Views on a TikTok video going up over the first week
  • Followers on Instagram increasing over months
💡 Sample project: Track how many messages are sent each day for 2 weeks. Show the busy days and slow days!

Cool things you can add:

  • When you hover over the line, it shows the exact number
  • Different colors for different types of data
  • A smooth line that shows the overall "trend" (is it going up or down?)
PERSON 2

📊 Comparisons

Your superpower: Show which thing is bigger, smaller, or better than the others.

What you'll create: A BAR CHART (like when you line up your friends by height)

Real-life examples you know:

  • Which fast-food restaurant is most popular in your school
  • Comparing test scores of different classes
  • How much screen time each social media app gets (TikTok vs. Instagram vs. YouTube)
  • Comparing heights of different basketball players
💡 Sample project: Compare how many messages are "Urgent," "Medium," and "Not Urgent." Show which type is most common!

Cool things you can add:

  • Color each bar differently to make it fun
  • Show numbers on top of each bar so people know the exact amount
  • Make bars horizontal or vertical—whatever looks best
PERSON 3

📌 Distribution Analysis

Your superpower: Show how many things fall into different groups or size ranges.

What you'll create: A HISTOGRAM (imagine sorting candy by weight or organizing people by age groups)

Real-life examples you know:

  • How many students got A's, B's, C's, D's, and F's on a test
  • Sorting movies by length (short, medium, long)
  • How many minutes people spend on homework (30 min, 60 min, 90 min, etc.)
  • Shoe sizes in your grade—how many wear size 6, 7, 8, 9, 10?
💡 Sample project: Look at how long messages are. How many are short (1-10 words)? Medium (11-20 words)? Long (21+ words)?

Cool things you can add:

  • Show the "most common" group in a different color
  • Add a line showing the average
  • Highlight anything unusual or surprising
PERSON 4

🎯 Correlations

Your superpower: Show if two things are connected or related to each other.

What you'll create: A SCATTER PLOT (dots scattered on a graph—like stars in the sky)

Real-life examples you know:

  • Do students who study more get higher grades? (probably yes!)
  • Do taller people weigh more? (usually yes)
  • Do videos with more likes also have more comments? (usually yes)
  • Do people who drink more water feel healthier? (maybe!)
💡 Sample project: For each message, check:
  • How long is it? (X axis)
  • How quick was the response time? (Y axis)
Do longer messages get slower responses? Let's see!

Cool things you can add:

  • Use a "best fit line" to show the overall relationship
  • Color the dots by a third thing (like urgency level)
  • Make bigger dots if something is super important
📚 New Word: A correlation means two things tend to change together. Like "more studying" correlates with "better grades."
PERSON 5

🍰 Segmentation

Your superpower: Show what percentage each group gets out of the total (like dividing a pizza into slices).

What you'll create: A PIE CHART or DONUT CHART (the kind of chart your parents use to show household budgets)

Real-life examples you know:

  • How your time is divided: school, sleep, homework, fun (24 hours total)
  • What percentage of your money goes to: snacks, games, savings
  • Different types of students: athletes, gamers, artists, bookworms
  • Your phone battery: Social media apps vs. games vs. school apps vs. other
💡 Sample project: Divide all messages into groups:
  • Messages about homework
  • Messages about fun things
  • Messages asking for help
  • Messages that are jokes
Show what slice each gets!

Cool things you can add:

  • Different colors for each slice
  • Show the percentage or number on each slice
  • Make it fancy by separating out one slice

📐 The Math Behind Each Chart (Don't Worry—It's Simple!)

PERSON 1: Trends Over Time - The Math

Math concept: You're plotting (X, Y) coordinates on a graph.

X-axis (horizontal): Time (day 1, day 2, day 3...)
Y-axis (vertical): Your measurement (messages sent)

Example with real numbers:

Day 1: 10 messages  →  Point at (1, 10)
Day 2: 15 messages  →  Point at (2, 15)
Day 3: 12 messages  →  Point at (3, 12)
Day 4: 18 messages  →  Point at (4, 18)

Connect the dots and you can SEE the trend!

Math concept: Comparing quantities using the same scale.

Example with real numbers:

Urgent messages:  15
Medium messages:  32
Low messages:     18

Make a bar for each one, and see which is tallest!

PERSON 3: Distribution - The Math

Math concept: Grouping data into "bins" (ranges) and counting.

Example with real numbers:

- Long (22-28 words):        23, 25            = 2 messages
- Very long (29+ words):     31                = 1 message

PERSON 4: Correlations - The Math

Math concept: Do two things change together? This is called "correlation".

Example with real numbers:

Message Length  | Response Time
5 words         | 2 minutes
12 words        | 5 minutes
8 words         | 3 minutes
23 words        | 8 minutes
15 words        | 6 minutes
31 words        | 10 minutes

Pattern: Longer messages → longer response times!

Visual:

Response
Time (min)
   10 |                    ●
    8 |              ●
    6 |        ●
    4 |     ●
    2 | ●
    0 |___________________●___
      0  5 10 15 20 25 30 35
        Message Length (words)

Notice: Dots go from bottom-left to top-right
= POSITIVE correlation!

Correlation Types:

  • Positive: Both go up together 📈 (like study time and grades)
  • Negative: One goes up, other goes down 📉 (like screen time and sleep)
  • No correlation: Random dots ⚫ (like shoe size and math grades)

PERSON 5: Segmentation - The Math

Math concept: Breaking a whole into parts and showing percentages.

Example with real numbers:

Total messages in one week: 100
- Homework-related:  35 messages
- Fun stuff:         25 messages
- Asking for help:   20 messages
- Jokes:             20 messages

Convert to percentages (part ÷ whole × 100):
- Homework: (35 ÷ 100) × 100 = 35%
- Fun:      (25 ÷ 100) × 100 = 25%
- Help:     (20 ÷ 100) × 100 = 20%
- Jokes:    (20 ÷ 100) × 100 = 20%

Check: 35 + 25 + 20 + 20 = 100% ✓

Visual:

        Homework 35%
       /            \
    Fun           Help
    25%           20%
      \          /
       \        /
        Jokes 20%

Pie chart divides the whole into slices!

Python is a programming language that's super beginner-friendly. It reads almost like English! Let me show you.

📊 PERSON 1: Line Chart (Trends Over Time) - Python Code 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Our data: messages sent each day
days = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7]
messages = [10, 15, 12, 18, 22, 20, 25]

# Create the line chart
plt.plot(days, messages, marker='o', color='blue', linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel('Day Number')
plt.ylabel('Number of Messages')
plt.title('Messages Over 1 Week')
plt.grid(True)  # Add helpful grid lines
plt.show()

# In English: "Plot day numbers on bottom, message counts on side,
# make it blue, connect dots with lines, add labels, and show it!"
What's happening?
  • import matplotlib = "Bring in a tool that makes charts"
  • days = [1, 2, 3...] = "Create a list of numbers"
  • plt.plot() = "Draw lines connecting the dots"
  • plt.show() = "Display the chart"
📊 PERSON 2: Bar Chart (Comparisons) - Python Code 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Our data: how many messages of each type
categories = ['Urgent', 'Medium', 'Low']
counts = [15, 32, 18]
colors = ['red', 'orange', 'green']

# Create the bar chart
plt.bar(categories, counts, color=colors)
plt.ylabel('Number of Messages')
plt.title('Messages by Urgency Level')
plt.show()

# In English: "Make bars for each category.
# Red bar for urgent, orange for medium, green for low."
📊 PERSON 3: Histogram (Distribution) - Python Code 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Our data: length of each message (in words)
message_lengths = [5, 12, 8, 23, 15, 9, 31, 7, 14, 11, 25, 6]

# Create histogram (bins = groups by size)
plt.hist(message_lengths, bins=5, color='purple', edgecolor='black')
plt.xlabel('Message Length (words)')
plt.ylabel('How Many Messages')
plt.title('Message Length Distribution')
plt.show()

# In English: "Sort all messages by length.
# Group them into 5 size ranges and count how many in each group."
📊 PERSON 4: Scatter Plot (Correlations) - Python Code 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Our data: message length vs response time
message_length = [5, 12, 8, 23, 15, 9, 31]
response_time = [2, 5, 3, 8, 6, 4, 10]  # in minutes

# Create scatter plot (dots instead of lines)
plt.scatter(message_length, response_time, color='blue', s=100)
plt.xlabel('Message Length (words)')
plt.ylabel('Response Time (minutes)')
plt.title('Does Longer = Slower Responses?')
plt.show()

# In English: "Dot for each message. Left-right = message length.
# Up-down = how long until they responded.
# If dots go up-right = correlation!"
📊 PERSON 5: Pie Chart (Segmentation) - Python Code 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Our data: message categories
categories = ['Homework', 'Fun', 'Help', 'Jokes']
sizes = [35, 25, 20, 20]  # percentages
colors = ['#ff9999', '#66b3ff', '#99ff99', '#ffcc99']

# Create pie chart
plt.pie(sizes, labels=categories, colors=colors, autopct='%1.1f%%')
plt.title('Types of Messages')
plt.show()

# In English: "Make pie slices. Each slice is a category.
# autopct shows the % inside each slice."

💻 How to Run Python Code (It's Easier Than You Think!)

Python code won't run by itself—you need to do 3 things:

Step 1: Install Python & Matplotlib

Python is a free tool you download once.

  1. Go to python.org and download Python 3.9 or newer
  2. Run the installer (click Next → Next → Install)
  3. Open Terminal (Mac) or Command Prompt (Windows)
  4. Type this command: 
    pip install matplotlib
  5. Wait for it to say "Successfully installed"
🤔 What's pip? It's a tool that downloads Python libraries (like matplotlib) for you. Think of it as "the app store for Python."

Step 2: Create Your Python File

Save your code with a special name so Python knows it's code.

  1. Open a text editor (or VS Code)
  2. Paste one of the code examples (like Person 1's line chart code)
  3. Save the file with a name like: my_chart.py (the .py part is important!)
  4. Remember where you saved it

Step 3: Run Your Code

Tell Python to run what you wrote.

  1. Open Terminal (Mac) or Command Prompt (Windows)
  2. Navigate to the folder where you saved your file: 
    cd ~/Desktop  (if saved on Desktop)
  3. Type: 
    python my_chart.py
  4. Press Enter and watch your chart appear! 🎉
💡 Troubleshooting:
  • "ModuleNotFoundError: No module named 'matplotlib'" → Run pip install matplotlib again
  • "command not found: python" → Python isn't installed yet
  • Nothing happens → Your computer opened the chart in a window behind other windows. Look for it!

⚠️ Common Mistakes (And How to Fix Them!)

Mistake 1: Wrong Colors Make It Hard to Read

❌ DON'T: Use neon green text on yellow background
✅ DO: Use high contrast (dark on light, or light on dark)

Mistake 2: Forgetting Labels

❌ DON'T: Show a chart with numbers but no explanation of what the numbers mean
✅ DO: Always include:
  • A title explaining what the chart shows
  • Labels on the X and Y axes
  • A legend if there are multiple colors/lines

Mistake 3: Too Many Chart Types Mixed Together

❌ DON'T: Make one chart that tries to be a line chart AND a bar chart AND a pie chart
✅ DO: Stick to ONE chart type per visualization. That's what makes it clear!

Mistake 4: Bad Data Makes Bad Charts

If your numbers are wrong, your chart will be wrong too. This is called "garbage in, garbage out."

✅ DO: Check your data before graphing it:
  • Are the numbers accurate?
  • Do they make sense? (Is one number WAY different from the others?)
  • Did you type anything wrong?

Mistake 5: Comparing Different Scales

Example: Comparing 5 students' test scores (0-100) with unemployment rates (0-10%). The scales are so different it looks weird.

✅ DO: Only compare things that are measured the same way. Or tell people on the chart that the scales are different!

🚀 Bonus: Advanced Challenges (For Students Who Want More!)

Challenge 1: Add Averages & Medians

Make your chart show not just the data, but also the AVERAGE (mean) or MIDDLE value (median).

Python code to find average: 
messages = [10, 15, 12, 18, 22]
average = sum(messages) / len(messages)
print(f"Average: {average}")  # Will print: 15.4

# sum adds them: 10+15+12+18+22 = 77
# len counts them: 5 numbers
# 77 ÷ 5 = 15.4

Challenge 2: Make It Interactive

Instead of static images, make charts where people can hover over data points and see the exact number.

Tools: Plotly, Bokeh, or D3.js let you make interactive charts.

Challenge 3: Combine Multiple Datasets

Track the SAME measurement over different groups or time periods.

Example: Show message types for Person 1 AND Person 2 on the same bar chart using different colors. 
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

person1 = [10, 15, 5]    # messages by category
person2 = [8, 20, 8]
categories = ['Urgent', 'Medium', 'Low']

# Make two bars for each category
x = [0, 1, 2]
plt.bar([p - 0.2 for p in x], person1, width=0.4, label='Person 1')
plt.bar([p + 0.2 for p in x], person2, width=0.4, label='Person 2')
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Challenge 4: Calculate Correlation Coefficient

Put a NUMBER on how strongly two things are related (from -1 to 1).

Python code: 
import numpy as np

# Does studying hours affect test score?
study_hours = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
test_scores = [60, 70, 75, 85, 90]

# Calculate correlation (will be close to 1 if related)
correlation = np.corrcoef(study_hours, test_scores)[0, 1]
print(f"Correlation: {correlation:.2f}")

# Result: 0.99 = VERY strong relationship!
# The closer to 1, the more related they are.

🧠 Understanding Your Data Better

Ask These Questions About Your Data:

  • What's the highest number? (Maximum)
  • What's the lowest number? (Minimum)
  • What's the middle number? (Median)
  • What's the average? (Mean)
  • Are there any patterns?
  • Is anything surprising?
  • What story does the data tell?
Example Analysis: 
Data: Messages per day: [5, 10, 8, 22, 3, 15, 12]

Maximum: 22 (busiest day!)
Minimum: 3 (slowest day)
Median: 10 (middle value when sorted)
Average: (5+10+8+22+3+15+12) ÷ 7 = 10.7

Story: "Messages vary a lot. One day 
we got 22, another day only 3. On average, 
about 11 messages per day. Most days are 
between 8-15 messages."

📐 Quick Math Reference Sheet

Common Formulas You'll Use:

Average (Mean):
Add all numbers ÷ How many numbers there are
Example: (10 + 20 + 30) ÷ 3 = 20

Median (Middle Value):
Sort numbers smallest to largest, pick the middle one
Example: 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 → Median is 7

Percentage:
(Part ÷ Whole) × 100
Example: (25 ÷ 100) × 100 = 25%

Total Range:
Maximum - Minimum
Example: 30 - 5 = 25 (so data ranges from 5 to 30)

Difference (Comparison):
First number - Second number
Example: 50 - 35 = 15 (first is 15 more than second)

Increase/Decrease Percentage:
(New - Old) ÷ Old × 100
Example: (150 - 100) ÷ 100 × 100 = 50% increase
