# 5-Minute Presentation Script
## "The Places That Need Clean Energy Most Are the Least Prepared"
### Team: Juan, Ismael, Kevin | Saturday Presentation

---

## FLOW: 5 slides, 1 minute each, Setup-Conflict-Resolution

---

### SLIDE 1 — THE HOOK (Ismael, 60 seconds)
**[Show: US map with power outage hotspots]**

> "In 2024, Americans experienced more power outages than any year in the last two decades. 83% of all outages are caused by the grid itself — not storms, not demand, the infrastructure just fails.

> The average American loses power 7 times a year. That's twice the rate of any other developed country. In some counties — rural Texas, parts of Louisiana, tribal lands in Arizona — people lose power 20, 30, even 50 times a year.

> Here's the contradiction: many of those same counties sit on some of the best solar and wind resources on the planet. Texas has the most wind energy potential in the country and also had the deadliest grid failure in modern US history — 246 people died in the 2021 freeze.

> The grid is failing where clean energy could help the most. That gap is what we're calling GridGap."

**[Click to next slide]**

---

### SLIDE 2 — THE DATA: WHERE THE GRID IS FAILING (Kevin, 60 seconds)
**[Show: Bar chart — Top 10 states by average outage hours per customer (SAIDI)]**

> "We pulled reliability data from the EIA — every utility in America is required to report how often and how long their customers lose power. The metric is called SAIDI — System Average Interruption Duration Index. It measures total outage hours per customer per year.

> The national average is about 8 hours per customer per year. But look at these states.

> Louisiana: 32 hours. Mississippi: 28 hours. West Virginia: 22 hours. Maine: 19 hours. These are not random — they're states with aging infrastructure, extreme weather exposure, and underinvestment in grid modernization.

> Now compare: Washington DC — 2 hours. Nebraska — 3 hours. States that invested in grid hardening and distributed energy are 10 times more reliable than the bottom.

> The gap between best and worst is a factor of 16. In the same country, same grid standards."

**[Click to next slide]**

---

### SLIDE 3 — THE CONFLICT: RENEWABLE POTENTIAL VS GRID REALITY (Juan, 60 seconds)
**[Show: Dual-axis chart — Outage hours vs solar/wind potential by state]**

> "Here's where it gets painful.

> When we overlay grid reliability data with renewable energy potential — solar irradiance and wind capacity factor — the pattern is clear: the states with the worst grids are sitting on the best renewable resources.

> Louisiana has world-class solar potential — 5.2 peak sun hours per day — but ranks 45th in solar deployment. They're burning natural gas while the sun beats down on them.

> Mississippi has strong wind corridors in the northern part of the state. Installed wind capacity? Zero megawatts.

> West Virginia averages 22 outage hours per year. They have mountaintop wind potential that could power the entire state three times over. Instead, 88% of their electricity still comes from coal — the same coal plants that are literally crumbling from age.

> The places that could benefit the most from renewables are the places investing the least. That's GridGap."

**[Click to next slide]**

---

### SLIDE 4 — THE RESOLUTION: WHAT CLOSING THE GAP LOOKS LIKE (Ismael, 60 seconds)
**[Show: Before/after comparison — counties that added solar+storage vs those that didn't]**

> "But we don't have to guess what happens when you close the gap. We have proof.

> Borrego Springs, California — a desert community that used to lose power constantly because it sits at the end of a single transmission line. In 2013 they installed a microgrid — solar panels, battery storage, smart switches. Outage hours dropped from 24 per year to under 2. Same community, same weather, same location.

> Babcock Ranch, Florida — built with solar and battery as the foundation. When Hurricane Ian hit in 2022, the entire surrounding area went dark. Babcock Ranch never lost power. Not for a second. 150 mph winds, and the lights stayed on.

> The DOE estimates that deploying distributed solar plus storage to the 500 most outage-prone counties would cut outage hours by 65% and save $18 billion per year in outage-related economic losses.

> This isn't about saving the planet — it's about keeping the lights on for the people who lose them most."

**[Click to next slide]**

---

### SLIDE 5 — THE CLOSE (Kevin, 60 seconds)
**[Show: The GridGap Index — ranked list of counties with worst reliability + highest renewable potential]**

> "So we built something. We call it the GridGap Index.

> It ranks every state by a simple formula: how bad is your grid reliability, times how good is your renewable potential, divided by how much renewable energy you've actually deployed.

> A high GridGap score means you have a terrible grid, amazing renewable resources, and you're doing almost nothing about it.

> The top 5 GridGap states: Louisiana, Mississippi, West Virginia, Alabama, and Kentucky. These aren't political talking points — they're data. EIA reliability reports, NREL solar and wind maps, actual deployment numbers.

> And here's the equity angle: those same 5 states have median household incomes 20-30% below the national average. The people paying the most for unreliable power are the people who can afford it the least.

> The grid is failing. The solution is literally shining down on the places that need it most. The data says close the gap.

> Thank you."

## IF THEY ASK QUESTIONS
- **"Isn't this a weather problem, not a grid problem?"** — Weather triggers outages, but the grid's age and design determine the impact. Japan gets hit by typhoons constantly and has 1/3 our outage rate. It's infrastructure, not nature.
- **"Why don't those states just upgrade their grid?"** — Cost. A full grid overhaul costs $500B-2T nationally. Distributed solar+storage can be deployed county by county for a fraction of that — and it generates revenue while it protects.
- **"What about nuclear?"** — Nuclear is great for baseload but takes 10-15 years to build. Solar+storage can be deployed in 6-12 months. The counties losing power today can't wait a decade.
- **"Is this a red state/blue state thing?"** — No. Texas is the #1 wind state and it's deep red. This is economics, not politics. The cheapest new electricity in every state is now solar or wind.
- **"Where's the data from?"** — All federal. EIA for reliability, NREL for renewable potential, DOE for cost projections. Public data, anyone can verify.
