# How to Pick the Right Chart - For Juan

---

## What Is This?

When you have numbers and you want to show them
to someone, you use a CHART. A chart is a picture
of your numbers. It makes the numbers easy to see.

But there are different kinds of charts.
If you pick the wrong one, nobody understands.
If you pick the right one, everybody goes whoa.

This tells you which one to pick. Every time.

---

## The 6 Kinds of Stories

Every chart tells a story. There are only 6 stories.
Figure out which story you are telling and the chart
picks itself.

### 1. WHO IS THE BIGGEST?
You have a list of things and want to know which one wins.

Example: Which state has the most batteries?
Answer: California has 12.5 GW. Texas has 8 GW.

Chart: BAR CHART
- Bars go sideways (horizontal)
- Biggest bar on top
- Smallest bar on bottom
- Put the number at the end of each bar

Think of it like a race. First place on top.


### 2. HOW DID IT CHANGE?
You have numbers that go up or down over time.

Example: Battery costs went from ,571 to 92 in 14 years.

Chart: LINE CHART
- Time goes left to right (2010, 2015, 2020, 2024)
- The number goes up and down
- You can SEE the trend

Think of it like a rollercoaster track.
You see the ups and downs.


### 3. HOW ARE THESE DIFFERENT?
You want to put two things next to each other.

Example: We HAVE 26 GW of batteries. We NEED 187 GW.

Chart: TWO BARS NEXT TO EACH OTHER
- One bar for what we have
- One bar for what we need
- The gap between them IS the story

Think of it like standing next to someone tall.
You can see the difference instantly.


### 4. WHAT IS IT MADE OF?
You have a total and want to show the pieces.

Example: 2,600 GW is waiting in line.
How much is solar? Wind? Storage?

Chart: STACKED BAR (NOT a pie chart)
- One big bar split into colored sections
- Each color is a piece of the total
- Biggest piece at the bottom

Think of it like a sandwich.
You can see each layer.


### 5. ARE THESE TWO THINGS CONNECTED?
You want to see if when one thing goes up the other does too.

Example: Do states that spend more money have more batteries?

Chart: SCATTER PLOT
- Each dot is one state
- Left to right = money spent
- Up and down = batteries installed
- If the dots go up-and-right, they are connected

Think of it like a star map.
Each star is a data point.


### 6. HOW IS IT SPREAD OUT?
You want to see if most things are big, small, or in the middle.

Example: Are most battery projects big or small?

Chart: HISTOGRAM
- Bars that touch each other
- Shows where most of the data piles up
- Tall bar = lots of things in that range

Think of it like sorting candy by size.
You can see which size you have the most of.

## The Rules (Follow These or Greg Will Notice)

YES DO THIS:
- Label everything. What is the bottom? What is the side?
- Add a title that says what the chart shows
- Sort bars biggest to smallest
- Write the important number ON the chart
- Use 3-4 colors max
- If it looks messy, remove stuff until it is clean

NEVER DO THIS:
- No pie charts (your eyes are bad at reading angles)
- No 3D (it makes the numbers look wrong)
- Do not cut the bottom of the chart (start at zero)
- Do not use a million colors
- Do not make people do math. Show the answer.

## How to Talk About Your Chart on Saturday

Use this formula every time:

"This chart shows _____."
"The big thing to notice is _____."
"This matters because _____."

Example:
"This chart shows how battery costs changed since 2010."
"The big thing to notice is they dropped 93 percent."
"This matters because batteries are now cheap enough
 to replace fossil fuels but we cannot plug them in."

Another one:
"This chart shows the top 5 states for batteries."
"The big thing to notice is California and Texas
 have 78 percent of everything."
"This matters because the rest of the country
 has almost nothing."

## Quick Cheat Sheet

| I want to show... | Use this |
|-------------------|----------|
| Who is biggest | Horizontal bar chart |
| How it changed over time | Line chart |
| Two things side by side | Grouped bar chart |
| What makes up a total | Stacked bar chart |
| If two things connect | Scatter plot |
| How data is spread out | Histogram |
